MICROBIOME PORTRAITURE

A public art intervention exploration of the relationship between microbiomes and the self.

The human microbiome is all of the bacteria, fungi and other single-celled organisms that live with, on, and inside us. This project was an exploration of this invisible, yet essential part of us.

The intention of this project was to represent the individual biomes as seen by the people who host them, something personal and interpretive in the vein of Humans of New York. The process was intended to illuminate the presence of one’s biome and have it represent a part of the self to the people who were drawing. By the end of the process they had spent time communing, however briefly, with a part of themselves that gets very little positive, personal attention.

Created in collaboration with Kelly Rio Saxton and Zoe Logan.

DON'T PANIC

A public art intervention addressing the rising sea level in New York City.

Rising sea levels brought on by climate change pose serious threats to coastal communities and natural resources worldwide. In New York, relative sea level rise is historically greater than the global average, and over half of New Yorkers currently live in marine coastal counties*. With a rapid ice melt scenario, sea levels could rise over 4 feet in coastline NYC by the 2080s. By making such projections more visible on our urban landscape, we hope to provoke discussion. The intent of this piece is to make the effects of climate change—which often seem so distant and abstract—more concrete and imaginable on our shared urban coastline by amplifying projections of sea level rise. We were inspired by this interactive map: Surging Seas. Later, it was used to determine where we would install our materials.

Read more about the project here.

Created in collaboration with Brian Clifton, Kristina Budelis, and Pedro Galvao.

HOME HARVEST

A modular vermicomposting and growing system that invites city-dwellers to engage more intimately in their food system.

Home Harvest is a modular vermicomposting and growing system that invites city-dwellers to engage more intimately in their food system. The goal of this project is to present the whole cycle from production to consumption to waste and back again. Although it may not be a strictly closed loop system, it will address and engage with all parts of the food cycle.

This concept would be sold as an easy-to-assemble kit with various add-on options and a fun and engaging assembly guide. Once the system is in place, simply add food scraps to the worm bin and the worms will transform the scraps into usable nutrients for the rest of the system. The hub of this system is a vermicomposting bin that is connected to a water reservoir that can then be connected to the other modules of the system.

This project is currently ongoing long-term user testing, stay tuned for the next iteration!

Created in collaboration with Josephine Quiocho.

THE FUTURE IS A GIFT

A craft and food guerilla pop-up fair at New York City's Union Square Park featuring a collection of projects that consider the future of our world in the anthropocene.

Co-conceived with a group of fellow graduate students at ITP, in a course titled Renatured, we spent the fall 2014 semester studying and making connections between humans, climate change, microbes, plants, bees, apartments, diseases, capitalism and ideologies. This culminated in The Future Is A Gift: a craft and food mini-fair in Union Square Park, strategically wedged between the seasonal Holiday Craft Fair and the Saturday Farmer’s Market.

Projects included: receiving edible coupons in order to partake, in exchange for having a photo taken (I Am an Ugly Animal); cricket cookies and jellyfish sorbet (Future Foods); Free DNA (“Give Yourself Away”); and a series of pills for the earth - encapsulated soil with seeds designed to remediate in specific ways, such as pollinator mixes, urban remediation and wetland stabilization (“Future Farmaceuticals”). For more about this project, click here.

A collaboration between Arielle Hein, Kristina Budelis, Brian Clifton, Sharon De La Cruz, Pierre Depaz, Pedro Galvao Cesar de Oliveira, Kate Godwin, Kyle Greenberg, Benjamin Gullard, Caitlin Marie-Noel Keller, Zoe Logan, Edwin Reed-Sanchez, Kelly Saxton, and Marina Zurkow.

TRASHY LOVE

A public art intervention that explores the relationship between people and their refuse by amplifying the emotional relationship that might exist between the two.

What if a landfill was an assemblage of broken hearts? What if every piece of garbage was a memory? In an emotional fallout between a person and their trash, what would be the reaction? And, if trash could get back at the person who threw it away, what would it say? These questions provided the framework to build a world in which we could explore the amplification of the emotional relationship between a person and their trash.

Our idea was simple. A staged emotional fallout as told by the ones who bore the brunt of emotional abuse in the relationship: the broken-hearted garbage cast out of the home and onto the street. To visualize this, we asked our peers to write short proclamations they’ve uttered during a breakup, or things they imagine they might say. We then cut those messages out of adhesive-backed vinyl, and on the evening before refuse collection, we applied them to an enormous pile of trash bags other discarded items. Located on Waverly St between Broadway and Greene St, the audience for this intervention included the many bystanders and passers-by, all witnesses of this ‘emotionally’ difficult time for the garbage. In the form of written messages, the trash publicly expressed feelings of sadness, anger, and heartbreak to its former lovers.

Created in collaboration with Brian Clifton and Pedro Galvao.

HIVE

An interactive spatial media experience inspired by the ecosystem and social life of honeybees.

Hive is an interactive experience that allows users to intimately engage with honeybees like never before. Conceptually, this project was inspired by the ecosystem and social life of honeybees, and it features a custom-built interactive art display.

Honeybees are the most highly-evolved eusocial species and they live in well-organized colonies that allow them to engage in a variety of complex tasks not practiced by the multitude of solitary insect species. Sadly, bee colonies are declining at an alarming rate. Contributing factors to this include habitat loss and alteration, introduced species, spread of disease and pesticide use. This project is an opportunity to celebrate the bees and their importance in our lives.

By forcing users to slow down, this experience provides the opportunity for a more conscientious and respectful interaction between species. Ultimately, leaving the user with a greater awareness and appreciation for the larger systems in which we all are a part of.

Built in openFrameworks using openCV and kinect. For further technical details on the project, see my blog post about how the project was created. If you're curious about the iterations we went through in this particular project, check out our initial concept presentation and then the final design plan.

Created in collaboration with Alina Balean.

FRIDGEBOT

A solution for managing food waste in communal refrigerators.

The Fridgebot is an attempt to eliminate student food waste in the communal refrigerator at ITP. By linking items placed in the refrigerator to students through their RFID-enabled NYU ID cardss, Fridgebot constantly monitors the state of the refrigerator and automatically send reminders when it senses food has been forgotten.

Fridgebot is a working prototype, currently being used on the floor at ITP. The project was featured at WebvisionsNYC in 2015.

Created in collaboration with John Farrell and Allison Ye.
